    1914-1918 - WWI

    Key Events

    • Caused by:

      • Nationalism in Europe
        • Identification with one’s own nation and support for it’s interests, especially the exclusion or detriment of the interests of other nations
      • The arms race
        • Naval arms race between the UK and Germany
        • British were concerned about the rapid increase in German naval power
          • This resulted in a competition of Dreadnought-class ships
        • 1909-1914
      • Tensions between countries
      • the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand of Austria and his wife Sophia by a Serbian nationalist group in Sarajevo, Bosnia, on June 28, 1914.
        • As a result, Austria declared war on Serbia, Russia declared war on Austria.
          • Then, Germany declared war on Russia
            • and England declared war on Germany.
              • Consequently, Canada entered the war because it was part of the British Empire.
      • Germany invasion of Belgium in August of 1914, in order to get to France, which caused Britain to join the war

    • The Prime Minister was Robert Borden, and he invoked the War Measures Act, which gave the government more control over political decisions.

      • This allowed the government to censor, and suppress communications
      • Canada refused to enlist certain groups like Japanese-Canadians because of their “strange ways.”
      • It also rejected Ukrainian-Canadians and German-Canadians because they were considered enemies due to their origins from Germany and the Austro-Hungarian Empire.
        • The government therefore imprisoned members of these two groups in prison camps.

    • The Military Service act was passed on the 29th of August 1917

      • It made all male citizens age 20 to 45 subject to conscription for military service
      • French Canadians opposed conscription as they felt they ahd no loyalty to either Britain or France

    • this war was the introduction to a new, more destructive type of warfare, trench warfare, because weapons had become more dangerous.

      • Provided an efficient way for soldiers to protect themselves against heavy firepower

    • the Treaty of Versailles officially marked the end of the war (June 28, 1919).

      • by the end of the war, 61,000 Canadians had died and 172,000 were wounded.

    Other

    • Farmers were initially supportive of the war but were then against it due to conscription taking away workers

    Main Parties

    • There were two camps in this war:
      • The Triple Entente, which consisted of Great Britain, France, and Russia,
        • Canada participated in the war because of Great Britain
        • The battles where Canada played a major role were Ypres, Vimy Ridge, and Passchendaele.
        • More than 600,000 Canadian soldiers enlisted and thousands of women served in the effort.
        • Most French-Canadians refused to participate in the war because they considered themselves only as Canadians and not as subjects of Great Britain.
      • The Triple Alliance, which consisted of the Austro-Hungarian Empire, Germany, and Italy.

    Afer WWI and the 20s

    Movements

    • Feminism
      • Women gained the right to vote.
      • Flappers who were women wearing short dresses and bobbed hairstyles, drinking alcohol, smoking, and driving their own cars.
        • War challenged traditional attitudes
        • Women took on more active roles
    • new types of live music
      • greatly influenced by African-American musicians like Louis Armstrong and later Oscar Peterson
    • new dances:
      • like the Charleston,
      • the Black Bottom,
      • and the Lindy, new fashion
    • The Group of Seven, Emily Carr, and Stephen Leacock promoted Canadian art and literature.
    • Associations
      • The Winnipeg General Strike of 1919 initiated the formation of unions for workers.
      • Indigenous people established associations to fight residential schools and discrimination.
      • African-Canadians also established associations like the UNIA in Montreal to provide services
    • 1,300,000 immigrants came to Canada.
    • Technology, industry, and the economy expanded, causing many people to move to cities. Many people bought luxury goods like cars, radios, and household appliances.

    Government

    • William Lyon Mackenzie King became Prime Minister in 1921.
    • Agnes Macphail became the first woman elected to the Canadian Parliament.
    • In the King-Byng Affair of 1926
      • The Conservatives accused the Liberals and William Lyon Mackenzie King of accepting bribes.
      • The Conservatives withdrew their support for the government.
      • King asked the Governor-General, Viscount Byng, to dissolve Parliament and call an election.
        • Byng refused.
          • Consequently, King resigned and protested this decision.
          • Eventually, Byng reversed his decision.
            • King promised Canadians to reduce ties with Great Britain. He won the 1926 election.
    • In 1925, the government adopted the Railway Agreement to allow Canadian railway companies to hire immigrants to develop the railways.
    • In 1928, the “Persons Case” recognized women as actual persons.
    • Canada began to establish closer relations with the United States.
    • The Statute of Westminster granted Canada its independence as a nation.
    • In 1929, the stock market crashed, and the Great Depression began.

    The Great Depression

    \

    • In 1929, the Depression began with “Black Tuesday.”

    • In 1939, Germany, under the leadership of Adolf Hitler, invaded Poland. Countries, including Canada, declared war on Germany. World War II began.

    Effects

    • All kinds of industries were affected. Many people in the United States and Canada lost their wealth and jobs.
      • Many people moved from region to region to find employment.
      • Many people had to sell possessions to buy necessities.
    • By 1933, exports had fallen by 67%, and 826,000 people were unemployed.
    • Farmers in Saskatchewan suffered from severe drought.
    • On April 23, 1937, 4,000 workers at General Motors in Oshawa went on strike for better conditions and union protection.

    Politics

    • William Lyon Mackenzie King lost the 1930 election. R.B. Bennett became the new Prime Minister of Canada.
    • Financial Crisis
      • The Canadian government introduced work camps for unemployed men.
      • Municipal governments started providing financial aid (called “dole” or “pogey”) to those in need. Many others refused it.
    • New deal
      • Prime Minister Bennett introduced the “New Deal” in 1935 (based on President Franklin Delano Roosevelt’s New Deal in the United States in 1932).
        • More progressive taxation system
        • A maximum work week
        • A minimum wage
        • Closer regulation of working conditions
        • Unemployment insurance
        • Health and accident insurance
        • A revised old-age pension
        • And agricultural support programs
        • In 1935, Prime Minister Bennett lost the election. William Lyon Mackenzie King became Prime Minister. The New Deal was terminated.
    • Two new political parties, the Cooperative Commonwealth Federation and the Social Credit Party, were formed.
    • Prime Minister King founded the Canadian Broadcasting Corporation (CBC) and the National Film Board of Canada.

    Canada WWII

    Main Parties

    • The two sides of this war were the Allies (Great Britain, France, and Russia)
    • The Axis Powers (Germany, Italy, and Japan). In 1941, the Japanese bombed Pearl Harbor, and the United States entered the war on the side of the Allies.
      • Germany conquered much of Europe except Great Britain and Sweden. Hitler attempted to conquer Great Britain in the Battle of Britain but failed.

    Politics

    • At the beginning of the war, Prime Minister King brought Canada into the war to provide resources to the Allies, not to supply Canadian soldiers.
    • In 1945, Ontario became the first province to approve the anti-racial discrimination law. African-Canadians gained the right to join unions, higher wages, and benefits.

    Key events

    • The Holocaust.
    • Germany invaded Russia
    • The war spread to the oceans.
    • The government imprisoned Canadians and immigrants of Japanese, German, and Italian origin in camps.
    • The Germans surrendered on May 7, 1945. Hitler committed suicide.
    • The liberation of Belgium and the Netherlands by the Canadian army.
    • The United States dropped two atomic bombs on the Japanese cities of Hiroshima and Nagasaki in August 1945. Japan surrendered on August 15, and the war ended.

    Battles

    • In 1942, the Battle of Dieppe ended in disaster for the Allies. 882 Canadian soldiers died, 587 were wounded, and 1,873 were taken as prisoners of war.
    • The D-Day battle in Normandy in 1944, where the Canadian army played a major role in securing victory for the Allies, pushing back the German front. The beginning of the defeat for Germany and the Axis Powers.

    Effects

    • The Canadian economy improved due to the industry and production created by the demand for goods and services required for the war.
    • This war was the most destructive in the world.

    Other

    • Blitzkrieg was a method employed by germany that relied on the combined use of armored units, to strike deep into enemy territory . It’s purpose was to create surprise, and overwhelm defenses through speed and fire power.
    • Caused by invasion of Poland by Nazi Germany, on september 1, 1939.

    1968-1983

    Politics

    • Pierre Trudeau became Prime Minister for the Liberals. He expanded human rights by promoting a “just society” and multiculturalism.
    • The government proposed the White Paper to Indigenous peoples.
    • In 1969, the Official Languages Act established English and French as the official languages of Canada.
    • In 1970, the October Crisis in Quebec by the FLQ (Front de libération du Québec). The use of the War Measures Act in Quebec by Trudeau.

    Movements

    • The popularization of counterculture and environmental consciousness.
    • The civil rights movement in the United States under the leadership of Martin Luther King Jr. and Malcolm X, among others.
      • This movement influenced demonstrations in Canada regarding civil rights, multiculturalism, and later the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms.
      • The recognition of Africville in Halifax as a heritage site of African-Canadians.
      • Many more immigrants came to Canada from the United States and the Caribbean.
    • In 1975, the Supreme Court decision in the Irene Murdoch case led to the establishment of family law reforms.
    • The government introduced price and wage controls to combat inflation. The end of the Vietnam War saw American military service refugees establish new lives in Canada.
    • In 1976, capital punishment was abolished. René Lévesque and the Parti Québécois won the Quebec election.
    • In 1977, the Human Rights Act was established. Bill 101 made French the only official language in Quebec.
    • In 1979, Trudeau and the Liberals lost the election to Joe Clark and the Conservatives, who formed a minority government.
    • In 1980, Clark’s Conservative government was defeated, and Trudeau and the Liberals returned to power with a majority government. The referendum on Quebec’s separation from Canada. The NO camp won, and Quebec remained a Canadian province. The Terry Fox run.
    • In 1982, the repatriation of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms and the Constitution established freedoms for Canadian citizens and gave more power to the provinces. Quebec is the only province that has not yet agreed to enter the Charter and Constitution.

    Key events

    • In 1972, the hockey summit series between Canada and Russia.
    • For some of the 1970s, Canada’s economy suffered due to:
      • the energy crisis
      • unemployment
      • inflation (particularly rising gas prices)
      • deficit.
    • The formation of Petro-Canada by the government enraged people in Western Canada, making them feel alienated.

    1984-Present

    Politics

    • In 1984, Pierre Trudeau retired from politics. John Turner became Prime Minister.
      • In that year’s election, Brian Mulroney became Prime Minister when the Conservatives won the election and formed a majority government.
        • Mulroney aimed to develop closer relations with the United States and modernize Canada’s economy.
    • In 1987, Mulroney proposed the Meech Lake Accord to bring Quebec into the Constitution by recognizing Quebec as a distinct society within Canada.
    • The defeat of the Meech Lake Accord led to Lucien Bouchard, a minister in the government, leaving the Conservatives to form the Bloc Québécois (a separatist federal political party) to promote Quebec’s separation.
    • In 1991, the government introduced the GST (Goods and Services Tax), angering many Canadians.
    • In 1992, Mulroney tried to address the defeat of Meech Lake by introducing the Charlottetown Accord to appease Quebec into accepting the Constitution. This accord was also defeated.
    • In 1993, due to his lack of popularity, Mulroney retired from politics, and Kim Campbell became the first woman to be Canada’s Prime Minister. However, the Conservatives lost the federal election and their status as an official party, winning only two seats. The Liberals won a majority government with Jean Chrétien as Prime Minister until 2003.
    • In 1995, the Quebec separation referendum took place. The separatist cause was defeated by a very narrow margin.
    • In 1999, the territory of Nunavut was established.
    • In 2000, Pierre Trudeau died.
    • In 2003, the Canadian Alliance and Progressive Conservative parties merged to form the new Conservative Party under Stephen Harper’s leadership.
    • In 2004, Paul Martin, a Liberal, became Prime Minister.
    • In 2006, the Conservatives won the election, and Stephen Harper became Prime Minister until 2015 (minority government from 2006 to 2011, majority government from 2011 to 2015).
    • In 2015, the Liberals won the election, and Justin Trudeau, the son of Pierre Trudeau, became Prime Minister with a majority government.
    • In 2018, the government legalized the recreational use of cannabis.
    • In 2019, the SNC-Lavalin affair saw Trudeau accused of interference by pressuring Jody Wilson-Raybould to prevent SNC-Lavalin from being convicted of corruption and fraud. Wilson-Raybould refused and left the Liberals. Trudeau and the Liberals retained their government by winning the election, but largely due to the SNC-Lavalin affair, it was a minority government.
    • In 2020, the COVID-19 crisis hit. Gabriel Wortman killed 22 people in Nova Scotia. Due to discriminatory events, civil rights groups like the Black Lives Matter movement successfully drew attention to systemic discrimination. The “We Charity” scandal led to the resignation of Finance Minister Bill Morneau. The Conservatives and the Bloc Québécois attempted to defeat the Liberal government in a no-confidence vote, but the NDP supported the government on the condition that it honored its commitment to provide more financial aid to Canadians in need. In the United States, Donald Trump lost the election to Joe Biden.
    • In 2021, Joe Biden officially became President of the United States. Kamala Harris became Vice President and the first woman to achieve this position.

    Movements

    • The arrival of modern technology replaced manual labor, causing job losses. More jobs developed in the service industry.
    • More women started attending school and pursuing careers.
    • The divorce rate and the number of single-parent families rose.
    • The birth of millennials, characterized by their technological knowledge.
    • Many more Canadians contributed to the arts and entertainment.
    • Canada made significant contributions to environmental awareness and peacekeeping.

    Key events

    • In 1989
      • Marc Lépine killed 14 women at the École Polytechnique in Montreal.
      • The Berlin Wall and the Iron Curtain fell, signaling the end of the Soviet Union and communism in Russia.
      • Free Trade Agreement between Canada and the United States was established. Canadians worried about negative repercussions, such as job losses caused by companies relocating to Mexico.
    • In 1990
      • The defeat of the Meech Lake Accord led to Lucien Bouchard, a minister in the government, leaving the Conservatives to form the Bloc Québécois (a separatist federal political party) to promote Quebec’s separation.
      • The Oka Crisis in Quebec saw Indigenous protests against the development of sacred lands. Canada’s economy was in recession, leading to job losses for many Canadians.
