    3 - Kingdoms Archaebacteria and Eubacteria

    3 - Kingdoms Archaebacteria and Eubacteria

    Nov 22, 20247 min read

    Bacteria

    Bacteria are single-celled, prokaryotic organisms. They live almost everywhere on Earth.

    Bacteria Structure

    Exterior

    The exterior of bacteria contains a flagellum (plr. flagella) which is a whip-like tail used for movement.

    The exterior of bacteria also contains pili (sin. pilus) which are hair-like protein tubes that attach to other bacteria for interaction. (They can also be used for motility (Not confirmed in class))

    Exoskeleton

    Some bacteria have a sticky protein cover over the cell wall called the capsule.

    Followed by that is the outer membrane which is a protective coating.

    Followed by that is the peptidoglycan layer only found in gram positive bacteria. (More on this later.)

    Info

    Together, the peptidoglycan layer and outer membrane make up the cell wall which is a rigid layer for support and stability and decides the shape of the bacterium.

    Followed by that is the plasma membrane or cell membrane which controls the entry and exit of molecules in the bacterium, maintaining a separate internal environment.

    Interior

    Inside the bacteria is filled with cytoplasm. The cytoplasm contains no organelles due to bacteria being prokaryotic, instead it contains single ribosomes for protein synthesis.

    Within the cytoplasm is the bacterium’s DNA which exists without a nucleus as bacteria are prokaryotic. (The DNA is located inside the nucleoid which is an irregularly shaped region where most of the genetic information exists (Not confirmed in class))

    Within the cytoplasm is also the plasmid which is a small loop of DNA that contains a few of the bacterium’s genes. This includes an antibiotic resistance gene that may develop from natural selection. The plasmid can only be passed to other bacteria via binary fission (asexual reproduction) and **conjugation (sexual gene exchange)*.





    Bacteria Shapes and Forms

    Bacteria come in 3 shapes:

    • Spherical - dubbed coccus (plr. cocci)
    • Rod shaped - dubbed bacillus (plr. bacilli)
    • Spiral - dubbed spirillum (plr. spirilla)

    After reproduction, bacteria can…

    • Stay as an individual cell
    • Form bacteria pairs - dubbed diplo
    • Form bacteria clusters - dubbed staphylo
    • Form bacteria strings/chains - dubbed strepto

    Info

    When naming bacteria shape/form, it’s the shape followed by the form in one word e.g. diplococc,i streptobacilli, staphylospirilla-(Doesn’t exist-hypothetical)





    Gram Staining

    When in contact with gram stain dye, some bacteria react differently than others.

    Gram-negative bacteria

    Gram-negative bacteria have a rather thin peptidoglycan layer in their cell wall. This results in them appearing pink when stained. Gram-negative bacteria are harder to treat than gram-positive. (e.g. Anthrax)

    Gram-positive bacteria

    Gram-positive bacteria have a rather thick peptidoglycan layer in their cell wall. This results in them appearing purple when stained. Gram-positive bacteria are easier to treat than gram-negative. (e.g. E. coli)





    Endospores

    An endospore is a thick wall only made by gram-positive bacteria. The endospore surrounds the cytoplasm and DNA. (In reality, not taught in class, it mainly surrounds the DNA with a small part of cytoplasm and ribosomes)

    Endospores arise at times when growth conditions become unfavorable for the bacterium. This is the dormant state of the bacterium and it can rest dormant for a long time through things such as drought, heat, and radiation until good conditions return.

    Bacteria Intake

    Nutrition

    Some bacteria are heterotrophic

    Some bacteria are photoautotrophic

    Some bacteria are photoheterotrophic

    Some bacteria are chemoautotrophic



    Respiration

    Some bacteria are obligate aerobic

    Some bacteria are obligate anaerobic

    Some bacteria are facultative anaerobic

    Bacteria Reproduction

    Bacteria reproduce asexually via Binary Fission

    1. Bacterium duplicates DNA/RNA

    2. Cytoplasmic membrane elongates, creating a bigger space between the DNA copies

    3. Cross wall/septum starts forming as the plasma membrane invaginates, similar to cytokinesis in mitosis

    4. *Cross wall/septum forms completely

    5. The bacterium has split into 2 identical daughter cells

    This is a quick and efficient process which takes ~20 minutes to complete.

    But this method of reproduction doesn’t promote genetic diversity, so bacteria have evolved to do transformation, conjugation, and transduction.

    Transformation

    The bacterium simply takes a piece of floating DNA from its environment (which may have come from dead bacteria) and integrate it into their own.

    Conjugation

    This is the only sexual process in bacteria which involves 2 bacteria joining together by a pilus, and one giving genetic information to the other (known as the donor and the recipient respectively.) Usually this involves the transfer of a plasmid.

    Transduction

    This is when a bacteriophage infects a bacterium and in the lytic cycle, accidentally gets a piece of bacterial DNA/RNA packaged into it when it breaks down. Then when the altered bacteriophage goes to infect another bacterium, it injects the previous victim’s DNA/RNA along with its own.

    Effects of Bacteria

    Benefits

    Bacteria are key decomposers in ecosystems that break down dead organisms and release carbon dioxide back into the atmosphere.

    Bacteria are key to nitrogen fixation, as some bacteria intake nitrogen directly from the air to make proteins (these bacteria usually live in nodules on roots of legumes)

    Bacteria are useful in making food and drink products such as cheese and yogurt.

    Bacteria are useful in recombinant DNA technology (manipulating DNA molecules) such as antibiotics.

    Harms

    Some pathogenic bacteria produce toxins that cause disease symptoms. One example is Listeria which is prominent in the elderly and AIDS patients. Listeria causes symptoms such as fever, vomiting, and diarrhea.

    Some bacteria are antibiotic resistant, with their resistance gene located on the plasmid. This is a direct result of evolution due to the invention of antibiotics.

    To help the antibiotic problem, we can:

    • Reduce the use of potent antibiotics
    • Finish the entire course of treatment for an ailment
    • Use other forms of disinfecting agents
    • Develop new antibiotics
    • Use vaccines

    Archaebacteria and Eubacteria

    Similarities

    • Prokaryotic
    • Unicellular
    • Both auto and heterotrophic
    • Reproduce asexually (binary fission)

    Differences

    The main difference between the two is that eubacteria cell walls contain peptidoglycan, whilst archaebacteria’s do not.

    Archaebacteria

    The kingdom archaebacteria contains organisms, many of which live and thrive in extreme environments where other organisms cannot survive. Some of these environments include swamps, hot springs, high acidity environments, and high salinity environments.

    Most archaebacteria are anaerobic.

    The fact that they can live in extreme conditions, which actually in fact resemble early Earth, suggests that they are very old.

    Types of Archaebacteria

    • Methanogens - live in low O environments (e.g. swamps, digestive tracts of mammals) and produce methane as a result of methanogenesis


    • Extreme halophiles - live in extremely salty water (e.g. Dead Sea, Great Salt Lake)


    • Extreme thermophiles - live at extremely high temperatures (e.g. volcanoes, hot springs)


    • Psychrophiles - live at extremely cold temperatures (e.g. Artic ocean, cold ocean depths)
