    1.1 Nucleic acid

    • There are 2 groups of nucleic acid 
    • DNA 
    • RNA

    Nucleotide consist of:

    1. Pentose sugar 
    2. Phosphate group
    3. Nitrogenous base 

    DNA: double stranded helix 

    1. Deoxyribonucleic acid 
    2. Phosphate group 
    3. 4 nitrogenous bases:  Adenine (A), Thymine (T), Guanine (G), Cytosine (C)

    RNA: Single stranded

    1. Ribose sugar 
    2. Phosphate 
    3. 4 Nitrogenous bases:  Adenine (A), Uracil (U), Guanine (G), Cytosine (C) Complementary base pairing  DNA: A-T, C-G RNA: A-U, C-G  E.g  A C T G T G A C 

    1.2 The cell cycle

    • In order to divide the parent cell into two identical daughter cells, the series of events that occur during cell division is known as the cell cycle
    • cell division is there in order to ensure the size of the cell remains small
    • different types of cells will take different amounts of time to divide The cell cycle The cell cycle is made up of 3 phases
    1. Interphase
    2. Mitosis
    3. Cytokinesis Interphase
    • longest phase of the cell cycle
    • divided into 3 parts: G1, S phase, and G2 G1 phase
    • Growth phase/ Gap 1
    • cell continues growing and carries on daily metabolic activities, such as cellular respiration S Phase
    • Synthesis phase
    • In this phase, the cell’s entire DNA instructions are duplicated so that each of the daughter cells can receive a complete set of DNA
    • During this phase the DNA appears to be very long, thin strands and are referred to as chromatin G2 Phase
    • Second growth phase
    • Cell continues to grow and organelles of the cell duplicates
    • DNA is also checked for accuracy and gets ready for mitosis

    1.3 Mitosis

    • As cells divide, they undergo a series of changes called mitosis
    • The process of nuclear division in which there is duplication and distribution of genetic information to form two new and identical nuclei
    • Each daughter cell has the same kind and number of chromosomes as the parent cell which produced it
    • Mitosis occurs in all body cells (aka somatic cells)
    • Every somatic cell in a organism has the same number of chromosomes
    • Humans have 46
    • After interphase, Mitosis begins Mitosis consists of 4 phases: Prophase, Telophase, Anaphase, and Metaphase (PMAT) **Prophase
    • During prophase, the nuclear membrane disappears and chromatin become form into duplicated chromosomes.
    • Chromosomes are composed of DNA and protein called nucleoproteins
    • These duplicated chromosomes are referred to as sister chromatids **Spindle Fibres
    • Each sister chromatid in a duplicated chromosomes is attached to one another by a structure called centromeres
    • Spindle fibres are attached to centrioles to serve as guilds for attachment and movement of the chromosomes. **Metaphase and Anaphase
    • Metaphase, centromeres of duplicated chromosomes attached to separate spindle fibres and line up at equator of the cell
    • During Anaphase, chromatids (one part of the chromosome, not the entire chromosomes) of each chromosomes separate and begin moving to the opposite poles of each cell **Telophase and Cytokinesis
    • In Telophase, the chromosomes finish moving towards opposite ends of the cell the formation of 2 daughter cells occurs
    • The cytoplasm divides. The cleavage of cytoplasm is called cytokinesis
    • Cleavage furrow - Basically the new formation of the cell membrane to finish off separating the 2 new daughter cells Extra Notes
    • In mitosis, the 46 chromatids are replicated (so there are 92 chromatids), which makes 46 chromosomes (remember that chromatid is the individual half of a chromosome)

    1.4 Meiosis

    • Meiosis - process by which sex cells or gametes are formed
    • Has 2 consecutive cycles of cell division
    • Instead of 46 chromosomes at the end, there are 23 **Set of Chromosomes
    • the four resulting gametes will have 23 chromosomes called haploid chromosomes number or (n)
    • Every other body cell has 46 chromosomes (or 2n)
    • you get genetic information from both parents (1 chromatid for each pair.) **Homologous chromosomes
    • 23 chromosomes recieved from father are matched by 23 chromosomes from mother to make 23 pairs of homologous chromosomes
    • They are similar in shape, size and gene arrangement **Recombination of Genes
    • Homologous chromosomes interact during meiosis (THEY DO NOT INTERACT IN MITOSIS)
    • Meiosis creates variation by two important processes: i) Cross over/Recombination ii) Random segregation of chromosomes. **Pairing of Homologous chromosomes
    • During crossing of Prophase 1, Synapsis occurs (pairing of homologous chromosomes)
    • the chromatids overlab and break and reattach to the other chromatid
    • This is called recombination **Genetic Variation
    • Chromosomes may move to different poles in Anaphase 1, resulting in different gametes
    • when haploid and haploid join together (Sperm + Egg cell), it produces a zygote
    • zygote will divide by mitosis to produce multicellular offspring Extra notes
    • In meiosis, during anaphase 1, the entire chromosome is moved to 1 pole, and during anaphase 2 the individual chromatids moved to opposite poles
    • This mean that after meiosis 1, 23 chromosomes are in each daughter cell
    • In meiosis 2, it functions more or less the same as mitosis, where the chromatids split up

    1.5 Meiosis as a Product of Reproduction

    • Asexual Reproduction: reproduction without having to find a mate Types of Asexual Reproduction:
    • Budding
    • Binary Fission
    • Vegetative reproduction
    • Vegetative growth
    • Parthenogenesis Some of the benefits of asexual reproduction include:
    • less energy required
    • only 1 parent needed
    • Rapid population growth **Why sex?
    • Sexual reproduction creates greater variation of traits which gives some individuals an increased chance of surviving changes in the environment Variation is produced by:
    1. Crossing over in prophase 1
    2. Random segregation of chromosomes in Metaphase 1
    • each pole receives a combination of patneral and maternal chromosomes
    1. Fertilization - different combinations of chromosomes and genes occur when two gametes unite **Gametogenesis
    • formation of sex cells during meiosis Spermatogenesis for males, Oogenesis for females **Spermatogenesis
    • Diploid cells in male testes undergo a cycle of mitosis to form primary spermatocyte (this is not meiosis)
    • Meiosis 1 turns the primary spermatocyte into two haploid secondary spermatocyte
    • Meiosis 2 turns the secondary spermatocyte into spermatids (these are immature sperm, will mature to become sperm over time) **Oogenesis
    • Oogonium turns into primary oocyte
    • the oocyte preforms meiosis 1 and form a polar body and secondary oocyte
    • Polar body gets discarded
    • Secondary oocyte produces an ovum and another polar body which gets discarded

    1.6 Abnormal Meiosis

    • Humans have 46 chromosomes arranged in 23 pairs
    • First 22 are called Autosomes
    • 1 pair is called Sex chromosomes, and they determine sex
    • Sex Chromosomes are designated “X” or “Y”
    • Karyotpes are the # and appearence of chromosomes in an organism
    • Used to test babies before brith for non disjunction disorders **Non-disjunction
    • Mistakes in meiosis
    • When 2 homologous chromosomes move to the same poll during meiosis
    • This results in 1 cell with a missing chromosomes and another one with an extra chromosome **Trisomy
    • Nondisjunction can also occur in mitosis where the centromeres don’t split, but it’s not really an issue, but in meiosis it affects the next generation
    • In humans, non disjunction produces gametes with 22 or 24 chromosomes
    • 24+23 chromosomes: 47 chromosomes
    • called a trisomy **Monosomy
    • When a 22 chromosome pairs with a normal 23 chromosome gamete, the zygote will have 45 chromosomes
    • Called a monosomy Non-Disjunction disorders Down Syndrome:
    • Trisomy 21 (3 chromosomes in homologous pair 21)
    • 47 chromosomes
    • 1/600 babies
    • risk increases with age of mother Turner’s syndrome:
    • Monosomic disorder in which there is only 1 X chromosome
    • 45 chromosomes
    • 1/3000 females Klinefelter Syndrome:
    • Trisomic disorder where zygote recieves 2 X and 1 Y chromosomes
    • 47 chromosomes
    • 1/500 babies Patau’s Syndrome:
    • Trisomic disorder in chromosome 13
    • 1/25000
    • many serious developmental problems, including brain, kidney, and heart defects
    • rarely live for more than a few months Edward’s Syndrome:
    • Trisomic disorder in chromsome 18
    • 1/6000
    • Many organ system defects
    • Barely live for more than a month, and even so most die before birth (miscarriage)
