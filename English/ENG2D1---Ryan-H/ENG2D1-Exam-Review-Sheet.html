    Part 1 review - Short story

    Literary Devices

    • Alliteration - a repetition of sound taken from the first consonant of a word
    • Allusion - a reference to something from history, mythology or literature
    • Connotation - feeling that are created by the association of words
    • Flashback - the writer describes and earlier time in the story that will help the reader understand the plot or character better
    • Foreshadowing - when the author hints at what will happen later in the story
    • Hyberbole - an over exaggeration that stretches the truth for entertainment
    • Imagery - used when the author describes something and appeals to one of more of the 5 senses
    • Juxtaposition - placing two ideas together so that their closeness and comparison creates a sharp contrast or a new meaning
    • Metaphor - a comparison between two different things that doesn’t use “like” or “as”
    • Mood - how a text makes you feel
    • Onomatopoeia - a word that sounds like the noise it makes
    • Parallel Construction - the repetition of a chosen grammatical form within a sentence
    • Personification - when an author gives human characteristics to an inanimate object (so anything but a human character or animal l think)
    • Pun - word play; when one world has an alternative meaning for comedic effect
    • Repetition - repeating a word, phrase, or line to add rhythm for emphasis
    • Rhetorical question - a question whose answer is already known or implied
    • Simile - indirect comparison between two things that uses “like” or “as”
    • Suspense - a feeling of uncertainty or curiosity created by the writer
    • Symbolism - physical objects, settings, events, and dialogue may all be used symbolically, that is to represent something more than what they literally are
    • Tone - author’s feeling towards a subject
    • Irony: Note there are 3 types of Irony
      • Dramatic Irony - this occurs when the audience knows something important that a character does not know
      • Situational Irony - this occurs when it is reasonable to expect a certain outcome, but the actual result is the opposite
      • Verbal Irony - this occurs when someone says something but means the opposite, similar to sarcasm

    Characterization

    Characters can be:

    • Individual - round, many sided and complex personalities (usually main character)
    • Developing - dynamic, many sided personalities that change, for better or worse, by the end of the story (may be antagonist)
    • Static - Stereotype, have one or two characteristics that never change and are emphasized There is also explicit (direct) and implicit (indirect) characterization
    • Explicit characterization - things the author directly tells the audience about what a character is like. e.g from Fish Cheeks:
    • Amy is in love
    • and was a teenager aged 14 at the time These are explicit characterization because they are told to use directly
    • Implicit Characterization - Things that we learn indirectly based on how characters act, speak, and think S.T.E.A.L method S - Speak T - Thoughts E - Effect on others A - Actions L - Looks For example in fish cheeks:
    • Amy is very emotional (because she cries when she learned Robert is coming for dinner, this is Action)
    • She is ashamed of her Chinese heritage (this is thoughts)

    Thematic statements

    • The theme of the story is the overall message about human nature or life that the author wants to convey.
    • Look at the title, symbols, allusions, and protagonists lessons for clues
    • Ask: what is the author saying or teaching us about the topic?

    Types of conflict

    Conflict is a struggle or clash between opposing characters, forces, or emotions. Without conflict, most plots would never go anywhere. Conflicts spur the action fo most fiction and non fiction and can create suspense. A story will usually one have 1 type of conflict There are two types of conflicts: external and internal External conflicts:

    • Human vs human
    • Human vs self
    • Human vs society
    • Human vs nature
    • Human vs supernatural
    • Human vs technology Internal conflicts - a character struggles with themselves and their opposing needs, desires, or emotions
    • Person vs self

    Narration/Perspective

    Point of view

    • First person: The story is told by a character in the story
    • Third person: The story is told by another character in the story or by someone outside the story who calls all the characters by “he”, “she”, or “they”
    • Omniscient - The story is told by a narrator who knows everything about all the characters in the story. they know all the characters thoughts, feeling, and motives and is free in time and place and to show or hide what he chooses
      • Objective Omniscient: Readers are shown what the characters do and say. There is no access to characters’ sensations, emotions, or thoughts because they can’t be observed from the outside.
      • Limited Omniscient this technique focuses on a single character’s internal and external experience. This enables a more intimate connection with the central point of view character and adds an element of suspense, because the reader knows only as much as the point of view character does.

    Part 2 Review - Paragraph writing

    Proper paragraph structure

    • A topic sentence
    • 3 points, examples, explanations (P.E.E) 
    • 3 specific pieces of supporting evidence, including at least one direct quote 
    • A concluding sentence
    • Correct spelling and grammar and punctuation 
    • Effective use of diction and sentence structure

    Notes on the Themes and Major Characters in Macbeth

    Majors themes in Macbeth

    • Appearance vs Reality: in Macbeth, this theme appears a lot as a lot of time, not everything appears as they are. For example, while Lady Macbeth seems like a nice woman, she has dark desires inside.
    • Ambition: ambition drives the characters a lot in Macbeth. One could argue that Lady Macbeth’s ambition to gain more power caused Macbeth to kill Duncan, leading to the events of the play.
    • The supernatural: The supernatural occurs a lot during the play, the most obvious of them being the witches’ supernatural abilities
    • Fate: In the play, Macbeth tends to always want to avoid his fate of getting overthrown, causing his paranoia. For example, he gets paranoid that Banquo might try to overthrow him, leading him to murder him. There is also a theory that Shakespeare added this to show that Macbeth was so paranoid that he sent a 3rd murdered to kill the first two murderers after they killed Banquo.
    • Guilt: Macbeth feels a lot of guilt throughout the play, such as when he talks about regretting killing the king. Guilt also plays a major role in the plot as Lady Macbeth apparently dies of suicide over not being able to handle her guilt because she knows she was the one who called upon spirits in order to get more masculine traits so she could gain power.

    Macbeth Major Characters

    • Macbeth: He is quite an ambitious character, and his ambition is so great that even thought he knows killing Duncan will probably lead to his own downfall, he does it anyway
    • Lady Macbeth: Macbeth’s wife. She is also very ambitious, and can be considered full of guilt, as she killed herself after not being able to handle the guilt of the role she played in Duncan’s death
    • Banquo: he is a kind and caring man, and loyal as well. The weird sisters say that he’ll never be a king but his descendants will, and Shakespeare probably did this because it was a rumour that King James (I) was possibly a descendant of Banquo. (Remember, it’s a political tragedy, it was made for the king so he wouldn’t make mistakes like Macbeth did)
    • Banquo is also as ambitious, but puts his honour to Scotland before himself. Because Banquo knows about the prophecies, he is a threat to Macbeth. When his ghost comes around, it symbolizes Macbeth’s guilt at what he has become.
    • Macduff: a noble man, who cares, above all else, the welfare of Scotland. He did not attend Macbeth’s banquet, foreshadowing his role in taking Macbeth down. Macduff suspects that Macbeth committed the murders to take over the throne for himself. He is also full of vengeance because Macbeth murdered his family.
